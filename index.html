Why Attendance Matters
3/21

Impact of Attendance on Pupil Outcomes

Research shows that pupils with no absence are 1.3 times more likely to achieve age-related expectations, and 2.8 times more likely to achieve greater depth than pupils with attendance below 95%.

Educational Impact

  • Regular attenders make better progress academically
  • Missing lessons creates gaps in learning
  • Higher attendance = higher achievement
  • Early patterns of attendance in primary school often continue into secondary education

Wider Benefits

  • Develops consistent learning habits
  • Builds social skills and relationships
  • Improves wellbeing and mental health
  • Acts as a safeguarding mechanism
  • Prepares for future employment
When discussing attendance with parents, use concrete examples of learning their child has missed rather than just percentages, which can seem abstract.

Attendance Data Tracking
8/21

Monitoring Attendance Effectively

Effective attendance tracking is essential for early identification of concerns and targeted interventions.

Monitoring Levels

  • 97%+ Excellent attendance
  • 95-96.9% Good attendance
  • 92-94.9% Requires improvement
  • 90-91.9% Concerning attendance
  • Below 90% Persistent absence
  • Below 50% Severe absence

Analysis Approaches

  • Daily tracking of absences
  • Weekly patterns (e.g., Monday/Friday absences)
  • Subject or session patterns
  • Absence code analysis
  • Group tracking (SEND, PP, EAL, etc.)
  • Benchmarking against national data

Key Reports from Bromcom MIS

  • Daily absence reports
  • Persistent absence tracker
  • Group analysis reports
  • Code analysis reports
  • Attendance trend analysis
  • Individual pupil attendance profiles
Look beyond just overall percentages - analyzing patterns by day of week, time of day, and specific lessons can reveal underlying issues that need addressing.

Assess-Plan-Do-Review Cycle
10/21

Systematic Approach to Attendance Support

ASSESS

  • Determine factors preventing regular attendance
  • Consider education, health, and care needs
  • Gather student and parent voice
  • Identify barriers to attendance
  • Analyze attendance patterns
  • Utilise a 'strengths-based' and 'trauma-informed' approach

PLAN

  • Develop Attendance Improvement Plan
  • Collaborate with student, parents, and staff
  • Set realistic timescales
  • Clearly define responsibilities
  • Share plan with all parties

DO

  • Implement the support plan
  • Ensure all are aware of their roles
  • Provide consistent support
  • Maintain regular contact
  • Document all interventions

REVIEW

  • Regularly review effectiveness
  • Seek feedback from student and family
  • Evaluate if desired outcomes achieved
  • Check all parties fulfilling their roles
  • Adjust plan based on findings
  • Consider escalation if necessary
Document each stage of the APDR cycle thoroughly as this evidence may be needed if attendance doesn't improve and formal steps become necessary.

Supporting EBSA
11/21

Emotionally Based School Avoidance

EBSA is different from truanting or 'willful' non-attendance as there is often an underlying presence of anxiety or emotional upset and no significant anti-social behavior.

Common Signs

  • Physical complaints (stomachaches, headaches)
  • Reluctance to separate from parents
  • Distress when preparing for school
  • Heightened anxiety in school
  • Difficulty with transitions or changes
  • Specific school-based fears

Support Strategies

  • 'Soft landing' in the morning
  • Consistent routines and visual timetables
  • Trusted adult check-ins
  • 'Reset moments' throughout the day
  • Gradual reintegration plans
  • Safe spaces within school

Resources Available

Consult with SENCO and Pastoral teams for access to:

  • EBSA Card Sort activity
  • Three Houses tool
  • Strengths & Difficulties questionnaires
  • Visual scaling resources
  • School Stress Survey
  • Ideal School activity
  • Attendance Improvement Plans
  • EBSA guidance documents
  • Sensory audit tools
Always remember EBSA is about anxiety, not defiance. A supportive, understanding approach is more effective than punitive measures.

Attendance Contracts
14/21

Formalizing Support Through Contracts

Attendance Contracts are formal written agreements between parents, school, and sometimes the Local Authority. They are not punitive but provide clear expectations, support, and accountability.

When to Use Contracts

  • When voluntary support hasn't worked
  • When parents are engaging but attendance isn't improving
  • Before considering legal intervention
  • To provide clarity on expectations
  • To document agreed support

Key Components

  • Attendance targets
  • School responsibilities and support
  • Parent commitments
  • Pupil commitments (age appropriate)
  • Review dates
  • Consequences of non-compliance

Process for Implementation

1
Invite parents to formal meeting to discuss contract
2
Collaboratively create contract with specific actions and timescales
3
All parties sign to confirm commitment
4
Regular reviews to monitor progress
5
Next steps if contract not successful (escalation or revision)
Always ensure contracts are specific, measurable, and achievable - vague terms make it difficult to monitor progress and compliance.

Legal Framework Overview

Understanding the Legal Context

Key Legislation

  • Education Act 1996
  • Education (Pupil Registration) Regulations 2024
  • Education (Penalty Notices) Regulations 2024
  • Children Act 1989
  • Anti-social Behaviour Act 2003

Parental Responsibilities

  • Ensure child receives efficient full-time education
  • Ensure regular attendance at registered school
  • Inform school of reason for any absence
  • Request permission for planned absences
  • Engage with support offered

Important Legal Considerations

Before pursuing any legal intervention, schools must follow the Attendance Legal Process Checklist as referenced in Sheffield's "Working Together to Improve School Attendance" guidance, 2024.

Legal Intervention Options

  • Penalty Notices
  • Education Supervision Orders
  • Prosecution under Section 444
  • Parenting Orders

Support-First Approach

  • Exhausting all support options first
  • Documenting support provided
  • Capturing parent and pupil voice
  • Demonstrating a graduated response
  • Showing how barriers were addressed
Always seek advice from the Inclusion & Attendance team before embarking on any legal process - they can provide guidance on appropriate next steps.

National Framework for Penalty Notices
16/21

DfE Framework (August 2024)

The new National Framework for Penalty Notices aims to create greater consistency in their use across England.

Key Changes

  • New national threshold for irregular attendance (10 sessions in 10 weeks)
  • Increased fine amounts
  • Each parent receives separate penalty notice for each child
  • Limit of 2 penalty notices in 3-year period before escalation
  • Mandatory consideration of penalty notices for term-time leave of 5+ days

Fine Structure

  • First offense: Â£80 per parent/per child if paid within 21 days, rising to Â£160 if paid within 28 days
  • Second offense (within 3 years): Â£160 per parent/per child with no reduction
  • Third offense (within 3 years): Direct to prosecution

Implementation

  • For term-time leave of 5+ days, home visits should be conducted by day 3 as specified in Sheffield Local Authority's attendance code of conduct to verify absence reason
  • For irregular attendance, penalty notices must be considered for 10+ sessions of unauthorised absence in 10 weeks
  • Sessions don't need to be consecutive
  • Can be a combination of different types of unauthorised absence (e.g., lateness and holiday)
  • Schools must demonstrate support offered before issuing notice
Always ensure parents understand that penalty notices are issued per parent, per child - this can result in substantial financial penalties for families with multiple children.

Penalty Notice Process
17/21

Practical Steps for Penalty Notices

Required Documentation

  • Attendance Legal Process checklist
  • Head Teacher's Certificate
  • Case Summary/Chronology
  • Evidence of support offered
  • Records of meetings with family
  • Pupil and parent voice evidence
  • Attendance register with comments

Process Steps

  • Consult with Inclusion & Attendance Social Worker
  • Prepare documentation package
  • Send all paperwork via Anycomms to LA Attendance Legal Team or the online PN Portal as directed by the LA
  • LA reviews and decides whether to issue notice
  • Monitor impact if notice issued
  • Consider next steps if attendance doesn't improve

Consider Statutory Defences

  • Authorised absence granted by Head Teacher
  • Sickness (with evidence) or unavoidable cause
  • Religious observance
  • Distance from school (if beyond statutory walking distance)
  • Parent's trade or business requires travel
  • SEND or EBSA considerations
Always check that all parents with parental responsibility are included in the penalty notice request. This includes non-resident parents who have day-to-day responsibility for the child's education.

Education Supervision Orders
18/21

Alternative Legal Intervention

An Education Supervision Order (ESO) is a court order that places a child under the supervision of the Local Authority, with the aim of ensuring they receive an appropriate education.

When to Consider an ESO

  • When parents are trying but struggling to secure attendance
  • When more intensive support is needed
  • When specific barriers exist that need addressing
  • Before prosecution in suitable cases
  • Where relationship between home and school has broken down

ESO Process

  • LA applies to Family Court
  • Court hearing with parents
  • Appointment of supervisor
  • Regular meetings with family
  • Directions given to parents and child
  • Initial 12-month duration (can be extended)

Benefits and Limitations

Benefits

  • Focus on support rather than punishment
  • Independent supervisor can help rebuild trust
  • Provides structure and accountability
  • Can prevent need for prosecution

Limitations

  • Requires court process
  • Limited enforcement powers
  • Relies on engagement
  • Resource intensive
ESOs can be particularly effective when parents want their child to attend but are struggling with specific barriers or have lost control of the situation.

Prosecution Pathway
20/21

Last Resort Intervention

Prosecution under Section 444(1) or 444(1A) of the Education Act 1996 is always a last resort when all other approaches have been exhausted.

Two Offence Types

  • Section 444(1): Simple offence of irregular attendance (max fine Â£1,000)
  • Section 444(1A): Knowing that child is not attending regularly and failing to ensure attendance (max fine Â£2,500 and/or imprisonment)

Evidence Required

  • Comprehensive attendance record
  • Documentation of all support offered
  • Records of communication with family
  • Actions taken to address barriers
  • Impact of non-attendance on education
  • APDR cycle evidence

Process Overview

1
Consultation with Inclusion & Attendance Social Worker
2
Preparation of evidence file
3
LA legal team review and decision
4
Court summons issued if proceeding
5
Court hearing and potential sentencing
Even when prosecution becomes necessary, continue to offer support to the family throughout and after the legal process to help improve attendance.

Ongoing Support

The Attendance Welfare Manager will conduct weekly supervision meetings with the Attendance Officer to highlight effectiveness of workflows, the school calendar, and target specific interventions.

Bi-weekly attendance review meetings take place between the Attendance Welfare Manager and the Assistant Principal to address specific strategic and operational issues and discuss implementation of new activities.

The Attendance Team attends a termly meeting with the Principal, Assistant Principal and School Manager to discuss the impact of whole school measures to improve attendance and punctuality.

The Attendance Welfare Manager in their role as Deputy DSL attends a weekly meeting with the DSL to ensure we implement our early help and early identification strategies with known vulnerable students and families and emerging cases.

These meetings ensure interventions are delivered proportionately, consistently and as early as possible.
